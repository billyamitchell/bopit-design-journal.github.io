Week 1

Tuesday

30 minute toy designs from childhood
I made a simple joint for two objects that was inspired by the tactile aspects of playing with legos I had as a kid. I wanted to replicate the *snap* that I found satisfying when I was younger and still do today. I used a plastic connection for the joint in order to provide the right feel to the point of connection. I used a soft mdf wood that was rounded off on one side to provide something that was nice to hold onto and manipulate the joint with your hands.
Class input:

  • It functions like a fidget toy
    • Has become enormously popular in the past few years
    • A tactile and satisfying way to distract your hands
    • Anxiety, a physical way of releasing some emotion
    • Visible to people around you
  • Make more ways of connecting
    • Different types of joints on the same object
    • Different forms of manipulating the joints
  • Sound
    • Moving and manipulating creates sound
    • Sound builds up with movements, story

We built a robotic creature with lego wedo, that has 4 wheels to move, and uses a sensor to perceive obstacles in its path. It was programmed using basic commands in scratch.
V1:

  • used two motors
  • could turn and navigate with all 4 arrow keys
  • fun interactive controls
  • Explore the room
V2:
  • used one motor and a distance sensor on the second port
  • Could only move forward and back
    • But, can sense obstacles in front, respond to obstacles
  • Further programming possibilities through scratch
Zanaya:
  • People playing against each other
  • Physical obstacles: things to drive over, etc
Possible gameplay: “Storm the Castle!” You control a robotic creature, on a path of destruction toward the castle where a bunch of evil landlords reside. But before you reach the castle, you encounter challenges in your path. Defeat your enemies and conquer the lands! On the board, the robot starts on the furthest space from the castle. Several enemies (standing paper cards) are placed on the spaces between the robot and the castle. In order to progress forward an reach the castle, each enemy must be knocked down using the projectile launcher on the robot. You can only use the launcher if you have defeated the enemy on the virtual encounter (short game on scratch). If the launcher is used and knocks over an enemy, the robot will still not progress because it needs the power it collects from the in game challenge (concept/game mechanic in progress)

Tuesday

ROBIONS GAMEPLAY:
Who is it for?

  • Young Adult (Age 15-18) -- or younger?
  • Interested in strategy games
What is it?
  • A turn based strategy game that takes place between 4 players/robots (1-4 people + AI) on a modular tile board.
  • The goal is to dominate the board and convert every robot to your side
    • There is a environmental/ecological component to the world that is illustrated on the tiles, woven into the story/goal, and visualized through the encounters.
    • The tiles have 2 groups: ecological and industrial
    • A “side” may constitute a ecological/industrial leaning faction of the robot.
  • Every turn, every player chooses their next move on the app interface. After everyone is finished, watch as all of the robots move at once on the board.
    • Players can move 1 tile at a time
    • Players can choose which direction if multiple directions are available
  • Upon conquering a new tile, the robot acquires resources from that tile based on the tile environment (e.g. if it is an eco tile, the robot acquires more eco related items and abilities)
    • If you encounter an enemy (other player) on the same tile, the environment of the tile gives and advantage to the respective items in player inventory.
Based on feedback:
Focus more on the physical gameplay. I think that the app could still be a part of this, but the focus should be on the game board.
